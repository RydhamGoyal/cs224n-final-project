Thine eyes I love, and they, as pitying me,
Knowing thy heart torment me with disdain,
Have put on black, and loving mourners be,
Which, yet sparkling from death
The eyes of truth so polished, shall not glance;
But I thought these day's call was
To show me the condition of my soul
As far as I can see them live.
My betrothed myself, I' the happy hope,
But basking in this gift long rests,
Nor all that feast day
Will outlive thee in thy sun;
For no sad love lies in thy love's form
Nor sorrow, to whom she shows flowers,
  To her eternal love can breathe.

So, now I have confessed that he is thine
And I myself am mortgaged to thy will,
Myself I'll forfeit, so that other mine judgment is not
Who used the bank on debts of robbers
And kings to benefit themselves.
But the deepest recompense thou art to my thief thou mayst,
And I myself pay this, not for what thy trust demands;
By which hast thou spent so much part,
That thou wilt not find what thou wilt steal?
Thy dwelling-place but be gone on thy books,
Shall never I remember my deeds,
Or perhaps, after we have turned back on all the good which thou hast brought
  As hard as I have had thee, I might be safe.

4
If thy soul check thee that I come so near,
Swear to thy blind soul that I was thy will,
And will, thy soul knows, is admitted there.
As I may in you live on my being born,
I will keep myself there though you sleep
So, if you shall flee, not worse from death
Than I of thy cheek I will bear there.
He that lives in thee breathes, you may bless,
And he that shals your crosse more grows
And betrays a blazon the shame of death.
  The conscience so loves your decease
  That I will do that which thou wilt not tell.


5
Thou blind fool, Love, what dost thou to mine eyes
That they behold and see not what they see?
They know what beauty is, see where it lies,
Yet they know no more to compare it with perfection;
They behold in what beauty thou perceive, it shapeth away.
But, if thou, on what end shouldst thou be
Who livest all eternity?
Such beauty, dost thou hate?
Suffy is thy beauty, but thou dost love some sight,
Some less or, if thou dearest, no more,
Being happy being nor happy, oft together far away.
  Such beauty I see on thee when thou art cold,
  When all men, the free, but gone, stay.


6
When my love swears that she is made of truth
I do believe her though I know she lies,
That she might think me some untutored youth,
Though my love hath made me a short old woman;
Which you do that only and oft lie,
To me my need of hope can assure.
Therefore, if I may in truth prove myself worthy
To add the fear of yours that, by grace, I possess
Then at least I am fair, decent, and worthy.
  When, truly, those I have beget my love's sickness,
  They are their excellence whose lives you pass away.


7
O, call not me to justify the wrong
That thy unkindness lays upon my heart;
Wound me not with thine eye but with thy tongue;
And tell me to my end, like unto thee,
O, as if I did base my love upon thee;
Though thy beauty be warred upon me,
That a number of thee say,
That thou art my husband, and that I, being accused,
Would not in this kind of blame thy hand.
What a fair crime! And then would my shame
Too bitter a stain, to live with thee so.

8
Be wise as thou art cruel; do not press
My tongue-tied patience with too much disdain,
Lest sorrow lend me words, and words express a malign opinion.
My ears can neither hear nor feel thine,
That rest my imagination to put thee in muse
Where my weakness must give my beauty to the eyes.
If you will then bear away thy art,
Make me mine subject.
If your love give thee favours which shall repay,
Then work thee, and first true art thou thyself,
But only if thy numbers in affection take off.
  In much the same wise as we both shalt,
  I believe myself most sharp, least cut of my being.


9
In faith, I do not love thee with mine eyes,
For they in thee a thousand errors note;
But 'tis my heart that loves what they despise,
And therefore, in mine eyes to see is taught
The dead it has long lived.
  But, woe to thee, I deem true truth rather;
  For, when I do reason from my life,
  I hate, and so do forgive,
  And whether thou forgive me or myself,
  To doth thy hate accomplish the inward end;
  By the force of good my mind may believe
  If I, worse than, love thee still.


10
Love is my sin, and thy dear virtue hate,
Hate of my sin, grounded on sinful loving.
O, but with mine compare thou thine own state,
Shall Thy righteousness measure down and praise.
Yet is it, where nature doth judge,
Thy mightiest in me than in thy husband's face;
Who
Thy love is to make me in thee, a pleasure
Of thy worth better than mine love;
And, having to use by thy friend,
To assure me a debt for your work is
Thou'st what thou loves in thy husband's bosom
And that thou and I are alike together.
  My sloth is in thy making, and thine sense
  And his goodness given me by thee make.
